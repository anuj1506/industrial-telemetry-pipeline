The current sensor in this dataset has a known low-end resolution limit: during sunrise and sunset, when power output is low (under roughly 1,600W), the sensor occasionally reports exactly 0.0 A even though current is genuinely flowing at a tiny, hard-to-measure level. To distinguish this normal sensor behavior from a real fault, three checks work together. First, an impossibility check flags any row where current, voltage, or frequency reads zero while real power is positive — these combinations cannot occur in a working system. Second, a percentile threshold (95th percentile of power output among known blind-spot readings) identifies cases where power has climbed high enough that a zero-current reading is no longer plausible, even within the blind-spot window. Third, a neighbor-based check, using pandas.shift() to compare each minute against the readings immediately before and after it, catches two additional anomaly types regardless of power level: a single-minute current dropout surrounded by normal readings, and a single-minute current spike occurring when both real and reactive power are at zero — a moment with no electrical justification for current to exist at all. Together, these checks flag 131 of 110,880 rows as likely sensor glitches, while preserving every row in the dataset for downstream analysis.
